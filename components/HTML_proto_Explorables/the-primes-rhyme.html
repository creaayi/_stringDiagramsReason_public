Act I

A table with a secret

Write the integers 1 through 36 in a table with 6 columns. Just a simple grid.

Now ask: which of these numbers can possibly be prime? A prime has no divisors except 1 and itself. So anything divisible by 2 or 3 is immediately out.

eliminated
prime candidate

Every number divisible by 2 sits in columns 2, 4, 6. Every multiple of 3 sits in column 3. What's left can only be in columns 1 and 5.

The observation: In a mod-6 table, prime candidates cluster exclusively in columns 1 and 5. Not randomly scattered — geometrically constrained.
Act II

Now fold it

Here's Mohammed's key insight. The table has a line of symmetry — down the middle, between columns 3 and 4. If you fold the right half over the left, something remarkable happens.

The two gold columns — columns 1 and 5 — land exactly on top of each other.

This isn't coincidence. Column 5 is the same as column −1 in mod-6. When you fold at the center, column 5 maps to column 1. The gold cells merge because they're the same residue class, mirrored.

The symmetry: The prime candidates in mod-6 come in pairs (1, 5) that are mirror images. Folding makes this literally visible — two columns become one.
Act II — continued

What if we sieve more?

Mod 6 uses the primes 2 and 3. What if we also eliminate multiples of 5? Then of 7? The table grows, but the same folding logic applies — and each time, the gold columns collapse perfectly.

Mod 6 = 2×3. Two prime candidates per row, folding into 1 column.

Each time you multiply the modulus by the next prime, the prime candidates thin out — but they never stop having this folding symmetry. The "rhyme" persists at every scale.

Act III

Where primes actually land

Now let's drop the actual primes onto the mod-6 table. Every prime greater than 3 must live in a gold column — but do all gold cells contain primes?

The distinction that matters: Every prime > 3 lives in a gold column. But most gold cells are composite. The table tells you where primes can be. It doesn't tell you which candidates are prime. Those are two different questions.

This is the honest boundary of the structural rhyme. The sieve constrains the territory. It doesn't fully determine the inhabitants.

What this means

The rhyme is real

What Mohammed's tables reveal is genuine: primes are not sprinkled randomly across the integers. They are structurally constrained — forced into specific columns at every primorial level, and those columns have a perfect folding symmetry that makes the constraint literally visible.

This is connected to deep mathematics: the wheel factorization, the Chinese Remainder Theorem, Euler's totient function. The symmetry under folding is not an observation — it's a theorem. It can be proved.

The formula: At primorial level p# (product of primes up to p), there are φ(p#) prime candidates per cycle, where φ is Euler's totient. They come in mirror pairs, so after folding: φ(p#) / 2 distinct columns. For mod 6: φ(6)/2 = 1. For mod 30: φ(30)/2 = 4. For mod 210: φ(210)/2 = 24.

Mohammed's paper goes further — proposing that this structure resolves several major open problems in number theory. Those claims await peer review. But the visual phenomenon he identified is beautiful, true, and underexplored as a teaching tool.

The fold animation you just played with is, to my knowledge, the first interactive version of this idea. Mohammed envisioned it as physical transparent layers — sheets of paper folding over each other. That image is exactly right.
